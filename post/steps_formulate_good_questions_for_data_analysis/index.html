5 steps to formulate good questions for data analysis

January 17, 2024 - 7 minutes read - 1307 words

In my years as a data scientist, I noticed how important and difficult it is to find the right questions. Data analysis is more fun when you know what to ask. Good questions make your results easier to understand, reduce the time needed for analysis, and lead to conclusions you can act upon. So how do you find them?

Let us take a practical exercise in which I will guide you on how to formulate questions for data science problems. I have a friend who is a big fan of Japanese cuisine and I wonder if “Japan: The Cookbook” by Nancy Singleton Hachisu is a good present for her. I will use data analysis to find out how much this book fits with her cooking and eating habits.

1. Set the analysis goal

Avoid doing data analysis without a clear goal. Not stating the goal leads to non-conclusive outcomes and lost time. The problem at hand is how to decide if the book fits the cook. My goal, as a data scientist, is to find the distinguishing and measurable properties of cookbooks by which I can make that decision.

2. Find your client and talk to them

Talk to the people who will use your analysis or data science product before you start developing it. You will discover that often their opinions and values differ from your intuition.

The client for this analysis is my friend to whom I want to present the book and the data is the book contents. My intent as a data scientist is to figure out the client’s perspective and by that to formulate the questions that are relevant to her.

To formulate the initial questions for the client, think about the story behind the use case. What steps does the client need to go through? What might influence them? Here is what I came up with: What is important for you when you are choosing a cookbook? Does the number of ingredients in a recipe matter? Does the number of recipes matter? Are you ok with using new ingredients? Anything else that comes to your mind on the subject?

These questions help to drive and structure the conversation. They are not the same as the questions that I will try answering during the analysis itself.

3. Rely on facts not opinions

Don’t expect to get concrete answers directly from your client. It’s the task of the data scientist and not the client to find actionable insights. From my experience, the dialog with the client goes somewhat like this:

Data scientist: What is important for you when you are choosing a cookbook?
Client: The recipes need to be healthy and easy enough, at least some of the ingredients should be easy to get.

Healthy and easy are personal judgments. They can have different meanings for the client and the analyst, leading to a misalignment between them. As a result of the misalignment, the analysis will be meaningless for the client.

So I need to find facts that describe the “healthy” and “easy”. Facts are formulated in measurable terms, everything that is not measurable is a personal judgment, not a fact. Continue searching for the facts:

Data scientist: Could you specify what is easy?
Client: Hm, don’t take too much time.
Data scientist: From your intuition, what is too much time?
Client: Oh, I don’t know, three hours, maybe.

People often struggle to put their intuition in numbers. To help them, provide example values and check their reaction:

Data scientist: What about two hours, is that too long?
Client: Yes, definitely too long.
Data scientist: What about one hour?
Client: That would work.

Now I know that the client will enjoy the book if the recipes take no more than one hour. Most likely not all of the recipes are that short unless the book title says “Recipes under 1 hour”. Therefore, the first question for my analysis is:

What percentage of recipes take no longer than 1 hour to cook?

4. Base your assumptions on the use case

The other term that was mentioned by the client was healthy. It is hard to find concrete descriptions for intuitive terms like this:

Data scientist: How would you describe healthy in this case? Amateur cook: Hmm, maybe they contain a lot of different vegetables?

It can happen that the client can’t describe what is “a lot” and even giving examples doesn’t work. In such cases, it helps to think about the process that the client goes through related to the task. The cook buys vegetables somewhere, so I can take the number of vegetable sorts that her favorite shop offers as the starting point. I visited my favorite market stall and counted the vegetables there, which turned out to be 30. To be on the safe side I’ll double that and formulate the next analysis question:

Does the book in total contain more than 60 vegetables?

5. Check for action

Decide on the actions that follow answering your data analysis questions before you get those answers. Otherwise, you may lose time only to figure out that you can’t make any conclusions based on the results. The action you choose to take should be related to your analysis goal. If you can’t find such action – don’t use the question in the analysis.

Above I formulated the following questions: Do the book recipes in total contain more than 60 vegetables? What percentage of recipes take no longer than 1 hour to cook?

The first one is easy to act upon. If the book contains less than 100 vegetables I call it non-fitting to the amateur cook’s tastes. Note that my action reflects the goal of finding properties that describe suitable cookbooks.

The second question needs more care though. If I already know the percentage of less-than-1-hour recipes, what would I make out of it? Here, the trick is to decide on the threshold value. I can start by saying that if at least 90% of the recipes are less-than-1-hour recipes, I conclude that the book is suitable.

In general, you can improve the thresholds by gathering feedback from your client, applying validation techniques or even basing on your intuition. But that’s a topic for another post.

By analogy to the approach above, I would continue the interview to figure out the rest of the questions and create an exhaustive description of what is important for the client concerning cookbooks.

Summary

To sum up, here are the steps to help you identify the right questions for your data analysis:

  1. Set a clear goal for the analysis, the questions you choose should help you reach it.

  2. Find out who is your client and talk to them. Based on the use case, formulate initial questions to guide the discussion.

  3. Formulate the analysis questions in terms of facts, not personal opinions. If non-measurable adjectives or adverbs arise, ask more questions to find a measurable description instead. The questions you could ask:
    How would you describe ____?
    From your intuition, what is ____?
    Could you specify ____?
    What about some number, is that ____?  
     
    A few examples to look out for: a lot, few, much, many, enough, bad, good, proper, decent, long, short.

  4. To find the facts, understand the process that the client goes through related to the use case that you are working on.

  5. When you have the questions for the analysis – decide if you can act upon the answers even before you attempt to obtain them.
